Often called the “nation’s report card,” the National Assessment of Educational Progress (NAEP) reading and mathematics main assessments are administered to a representative sample of fourth and eighth graders in each state every two years. NAEP scores offer something rare in education policy: data that are standardized across states and across time. Policymakers and pundits seize upon these data as evidence to support their preferred policies. But comparing NAEP scores assumes that states serve the same students —and we know they don’t. Schools in Massachusetts serve a different student population than schools in Mississippi, and the student population in 2015 looks different from the student population in 1996.

In a report released last year, we proposed a better way to compare and talk about NAEP scores. The report calculated adjusted NAEP scores by accounting for demographic differences across students in each state. These adjusted scores allow for students to be compared with their demographically similar peers using factors such as race, receipt of special education services, and status as an English language learner. These are factors we know can affect test results, yet they are not shown in NAEP scores. By accounting for these characteristics, our adjusted NAEP scores help to isolate the effects of differences in policies and practices across states.

The interactive tool below brings those adjusted NAEP scores to life. For all 50 states and DC, you can see the adjusted and unadjusted NAEP scores for the various tests and grades, as well as how those scores change over time. Using the drop-down menus, you can choose which factors are accounted for in the adjusted score, examining how controlling for different characteristics affect different states. What’s clear, no matter how you adjust the data, is that there is significant variation among state outcomes, even after standardizing student demographics.

About the data

The adjusted scores are based on analysis of student-level, restricted-use NAEP data from the US Department of Education.

The information presented here is the result of analysis of the student-level data from the 1996, 1998, 2002, 2002, 2003, 2005, 2007, 2009, 2011, and 2013 administrations of the NAEP assessments. Though 2015 NAEP scores have been released, the student-level data needed to make demographic adjustments is not yet available to researchers. To forecast a 2015 adjustment, we assume that the demographics of the state are the same as in the previous testing year and apply the 2013 adjustment—the difference between unadjusted and adjusted scores in 2013—to the raw score.

We perform an adjustment across states and time by estimating the relationship between student characteristics and NAEP scores using the student-level data from 2003 (roughly the midpoint of the data). Our adjusted scores for each state reflect the difference between predicted student performance and actual student performance. The details of the data and methodology are provided in the technical appendix.
